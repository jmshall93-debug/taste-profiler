# Recommendations — Soul Harmony Guide

*Session: March 2026*

---

## Film

**Stalker — Andrei Tarkovsky (1979)**
A man guides two others into a forbidden zone where the laws of reality soften and something like a wish-granting presence waits at the centre. Tarkovsky shoots in long, unbroken takes through ruined industrial landscapes that become inexplicably sacred — this is Twin Peaks' Black Lodge and the Spinozist wilderness and the infinite veil made into cinema. The Zone never explains itself. You will feel it like a physical pressure.

**Picnic at Hanging Rock — Peter Weir (1975)**
A group of schoolgirls walk into an ancient volcanic formation in Australia on Valentine's Day and some of them do not return. Weir never explains what happened. The landscape — vast, indifferent, humming with something pre-human — is the protagonist. It has the Twin Peaks quality of mystery that must not be resolved, combined with the wilderness sublime you're reaching toward.

**The Tree of Life — Terrence Malick (2011)**
It opens with the formation of the cosmos and the appearance of the first life — a cinematic depiction of abiogenesis. It then narrows to a single family in 1950s Texas and holds those two scales — the cosmic and the intimate — in the same frame, drenched in golden light. Malick is doing visually what Spinoza did philosophically. You have likely avoided this; don't.

## Essay Film / Documentary

**Sans Soleil — Chris Marker (1983)**
A cameraman's letters, read aloud by a woman's voice, moving between Japan, Guinea-Bissau, and Iceland. It's about memory, time, and what images do to reality — whether a photograph preserves a moment or replaces it. Marker was asking the questions Baudrillard was writing about at exactly the same time, but through footage of street festivals, airport waiting rooms, and a cat cemetery in Tokyo. The essay film that Adam Curtis is working in the shadow of. Quiet, strange, and completely unlike anything else.

**Nostalgia for the Light — Patricio Guzmán (2010)**
In the Atacama desert — the driest place on Earth — astronomers point telescopes at the origins of the universe while women nearby search the same ground for the bones of those disappeared by Pinochet. Guzmán holds both searches in the same frame without forcing them together. Deep time, archaeology, the cosmos, political memory, the sublime and the horrific occupying the same landscape. Connects directly to the Apollo 11 interest in the vast scale of human endeavour, and to the hidden-knowledge thread — except here what's buried is not ancient but recent, and not yet found.

## Books

**The Passion According to G.H. — Clarice Lispector**
A Brazilian woman, alone in her apartment, crushes a cockroach and has a complete dissolution of the self into pure immanence. This sounds absurd; it is devastating. Lispector writes prose like consciousness under a microscope — psychedelic without chemicals, Spinozist without argument, the abiogenesis moment rendered in language. The closest thing in literature to what you described as "the infinite veil."

**The Living Mountain — Nan Shepherd**
Written in the 1940s about the Cairngorms — not the Alps, not the Rockies, but mountains you could stand on within a few hours of wherever you are. Shepherd goes into them not to conquer but to be taught. She writes about granite and cold water and the particular quality of mountain light with the precision of a mystic and the discipline of a scientist. This is the book that might change your relationship to the landscape you actually live near.

**The Rings of Saturn — W.G. Sebald**
A man walks through Suffolk — the most flat, managed, domesticated English landscape imaginable — and finds it saturated with hidden history, ruins, catastrophe, the past refusing to stay buried. Sebald writes about the Silk Road, the herring trade, Thomas Browne, colonial violence, all braided together in long melancholy paragraphs. He does for landscape what Baudrillard does for culture; the ordinary English countryside becomes archaeological, uncanny.

**Underland — Robert Macfarlane**
Macfarlane goes underground — into caves, ice shelves, mycorrhizal networks, Chernobyl's exclusion zone, ancient burial chambers — and writes about deep time, hidden knowledge, the archaeology of the not-yet-discovered. Ancient wisdom, artifacts, hidden knowledge, the earth as archive. He has also spent years finding the wilderness that exists, quietly, in Britain.

**The Unconsoled — Kazuo Ishiguro**
Not his famous one. A pianist arrives in a European city and the world keeps slipping its moorings — obligations multiply, rooms lead to impossible places, time behaves wrongly. The infinite veil as literary form. More unsettling and strange than anything he's known for.

**Rumi — The Masnavi (trans. Jawid Mojaddedi)**
Not the airport Rumi. The actual Masnavi is Dostoevskian in scope — stories within stories, philosophy and grief and divine longing all braided. Ancient wisdom that has genuinely earned the name. The Brothers K's spiritual wrestling in a different tradition.

## Music

### Ambient / Electronic / Drone

**Grouper — Dragging a Dead Deer Up a Hill**
Folk-drone that sounds like someone singing from underwater — or from the other side of the membrane. Sits in the same territory as Talk Talk's *Laughing Stock* but colder and more solitary, as though the sacred has been stripped of warmth and only the silence remains. If Talk Talk opened something and BOC gave you the ache of half-remembered things, Grouper is what lives between them.

**Stars of the Lid — And Their Refinement of the Decline**
Orchestral ambient that does what Talk Talk does in a different register — vast, slow accumulation, then something emotionally overwhelming arriving without warning. The wilderness you're longing for, in sound.

**Lustmord — Heresy**
Dark ambient at its most geological. Where Boards of Canada makes you nostalgic for a lost childhood, Lustmord makes you feel the age of rock itself. The cold sublime. Something that genuinely doesn't care about you.

### Sacramental / Ritual / Peaceful with Light Percussion

**Jon Hassell & Brian Eno — Possible Musics (1980)**
"Fourth World" music — ambient textures with organic, lightly tribal percussion from African and Indonesian traditions. Processed trumpet drifts over soft, repetitive drumming that never resolves. The space it creates is genuinely sacred in character. His solo record *Dream Theory in Malaya* (1981) goes deeper into this territory. These recordings influenced almost every ambient and electronic artist who came after.

**Midori Takada — Through the Looking Glass (1983)**
Japanese percussionist, solo marimba and hand drums. The most direct answer to "peaceful, almost ambient, with light rhythmic drumming." The marimba creates resonant, sustained tones that blur into drone; the percussion underneath is soft, patient, unhurried. Sounds ancient without imitating anything specific.

**Alice Coltrane — World Spirituality Classics Vol. 1 (recorded 1982–95)**
Made for her Vedantic ashram in California — recordings never intended for commercial release. Organ drones, light hand percussion, devotional vocals. Genuinely sacramental in the most direct sense: music made *as* a spiritual practice, not music *about* one. *Turiya Sings* (2019) is the companion piece — her voice alone over organ.

**Popol Vuh — Hosianna Mantra (1972)**
Werner Herzog's composers. Female soprano, organ, oboe, and tabla played softly throughout. It sounds like medieval sacred music being played in a room where the walls have dissolved. *Letzte Tage — Letzte Nächte* (1976) is more ambient and equally beautiful.

**Laraaji — Day of Radiance (1980)**
Produced by Eno as part of his *Ambient* series. Laraaji plays electric zither in a state of sustained spiritual practice — a genuine contemplative, not a performer playing at it. The instrument creates shimmering, overlapping tones; the rhythm is light and cyclical. Peaceful without being inert.

**Hariprasad Chaurasia**
Indian bansuri (bamboo flute) master. The bansuri's pure tone is the closest thing to a human voice without being one — intimate, meditative, deeply spiritual. The slow *alap* sections build into gentle rhythmic interplay with the tabla. Start with *Call of the Valley* (1967) — recorded with Shivkumar Sharma on santoor.

**Steve Reich — Music for 18 Musicians (1976)**
Minimal patterns on mallet instruments and voices, cycling in and out of phase for over an hour. Light, constant, meditative pulse. The most secular item in this section but perhaps the most purely trance-inducing.

### Sacred / Ancient

**Jan Garbarek — Officium**
Norwegian saxophonist playing across the Hilliard Ensemble's medieval sacred polyphony. It sounds like the Briggs scene feels — ordinary space suddenly vast, a presence arriving uninvited. Ancient and contemporary and genuinely strange.

### African Spiritual / Percussive

**Maalem Mahmoud Guinia — Colours of the Night**
Gnawa — a Moroccan healing tradition descended from enslaved West Africans. The guembri (bass lute) creates a deep, woody, almost subsonic tone layered over iron castanets and chanting. The ceremonies last all night and are designed to invoke spirits and enter trance states. Music made specifically to thin the membrane. *The Trance of Seven Colours* (with Pharoah Sanders) is the more accessible companion.

**Mulatu Astatke — Ethio Jazz (1974)**
Ethiopian jazz from the 1960s–70s, fusing jazz and Latin music with the ancient Ethiopian pentatonic scale. Simultaneously ancient and modern, deeply minor-key, hypnotic, slightly wrong in a way that keeps pulling you back. *Ethiopiques Vol. 4* is the essential collection. Start there, then the broader *Ethiopiques* series (30+ volumes of archival recordings).

**Hailu Mergia — Hailu Mergia & His Classical Instrument (1985)**
Ethiopian keyboardist playing ancient Ethiopian melodies alone on accordion. Modal, slow, deeply interior. The accordion transforms the Ethiopian pentatonic scale into something that sounds like it's coming from inside a very old building.

**Tinariwen — Amassakoul (2004)**
Tuareg nomads from the Saharan desert playing electric guitars. The music descends directly from the same West African roots as American blues — hearing it, you understand viscerally that the blues was always desert music, always music about immense empty space. Melancholy, hypnotic, vast. *Aman Iman* (2007) follows naturally.

**Baka Forest People — Heart of the Forest**
Polyphonic forest singing from Cameroon and Congo — layered, interlocking voices that sound like the forest thinking. Creates harmonics that no single voice produces. Music that sounds like emergence, like abiogenesis in sound. Connects directly to abiogenesis, the oneness of all, consciousness before individuation.

**Konono N°1 — Congotronics (2004)**
Electric thumb piano amplified through handmade microphones built from car parts, run through distorted PA systems. Ancient ritual music processed through broken electronics — lo-fi, buzzing, distorted, relentlessly hypnotic. The raw material that artists like Björk and Four Tet have circled around for years.

**Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan — The Last Prophet (1994)**
Pakistani Sufi qawwali — a direct cousin of Gnawa, both descended from the same West African spiritual music carried along trade routes. The WOMAD concert recordings. Start with the opening of disc one — a slow, extended *alap* that builds with extraordinary patience. The most sacramental music ever committed to tape.

## Thinkers & Ideas

**Teilhard de Chardin — The Phenomenon of Man**
A Jesuit paleontologist who spent his life digging up ancient bones in China and Central Asia and concluded that evolution, abiogenesis, and the sacred were a single phenomenon. He mapped consciousness onto the cosmos — the universe slowly becoming aware of itself. Condemned by the Church and dismissed by some scientists, which is always a promising sign. Spinoza with deep time added and the smell of excavation sites on it.

**Simone Weil**
Philosopher, mystic, activist. Her concept of *decreation* — the self deliberately emptying to become pure attention — is Spinozism arrived at through suffering rather than reason. *Waiting for God* is one of the most honest books about the sacred ever written.

**Alfred North Whitehead — Process Philosophy**
Spinozism's twentieth-century heir, and the most coherent philosophical framework for what abiogenesis and the Great Filter are actually pointing at. Reality as process rather than substance — everything alive, everything relational. Strangely underread given how well it fits the current moment.

### Nietzsche — Suggested Reading Order

1. **The Gay Science** — Start here. Aphoristic, musical, joyful. Section 125 ("The Madman") is where "God is dead" appears in full context — not triumphant, but terrified. Book IV (*Sanctus Januarius*) is where his most beautiful thinking lives.

2. **On the Genealogy of Morality** — His finest sustained argument. Three essays on where values come from, the origin of guilt, and the nature of the ascetic ideal. Essay III bridges directly to Baudrillard: the ascetic ideal as civilisation's operating system, meaning manufactured from negation.

3. **Thus Spoke Zarathustra** — Read as poetry, not doctrine. With the above behind you, the eternal recurrence passages and the Übermensch sections land as lived challenge rather than ideology. The chapter "On the Vision and the Riddle" is the eternal recurrence at its most visceral.

4. **The Birth of Tragedy** *(go backwards)* — His first book, written young. The Apollonian/Dionysian distinction maps directly onto your aesthetic life. Twin Peaks holds both. Talk Talk is almost purely Dionysian. Lawrence of Arabia is Apollo in the service of something Dionysian underneath.

5. **Twilight of the Idols + The Antichrist** *(together, as a coda)* — Short, ferocious, written near the end of his sane life. Every major Western idol tapped and found hollow. Read in a weekend.

*Secondary text: Alexander Nehamas — Nietzsche: Life as Literature. Reads Nietzsche as an aesthetic project — the task of making one's life a work of art.*

## Visual Art

**Samuel Palmer**
Blake's most gifted disciple. In the 1820s, in a small valley in Kent, Palmer painted the English pastoral as though it were on the edge of revelation — harvest moons burning orange over ancient oaks, shepherds in a light that is not quite natural, the English countryside made numinous and slightly terrifying. He was painting what you described as wanting from the UK landscape: wildness and depth in the place you actually live. His work is in the Ashmolean and the V&A; you could stand in front of it this week.

## Places

### UK & The North Atlantic

**The Outer Hebrides — Lewis & Harris**
The most atmospheric landscape in the British Isles. The Callanish standing stones on Lewis are older than Stonehenge and almost entirely unvisited — you can stand among them alone at dawn with no fence, no ticket office, no interpretation board. The beaches at Harris (Luskentyre, Scarista) are white sand and turquoise water under a vast Atlantic sky. The light here is genuinely unlike anywhere else in Britain — low, shifting, silver. Ancient wisdom literally embedded in stone, and the wilderness you've been reaching for within a two-hour flight.

**The Cairngorms**
Nan Shepherd's mountains. Not dramatic peaks but a genuine high plateau — the largest arctic-alpine landscape in Britain — where the weather is serious and the scale is real. Read *The Living Mountain* first. The book will teach you how to be there rather than merely look at it.

**Orkney**
One of the most archaeologically dense places on Earth, worn lightly. Skara Brae is a Neolithic village older than the pyramids, still emerging from the sand dunes. Maeshowe is a passage tomb aligned precisely with the winter solstice — 5,000 years ago someone calculated the exact angle. The Ring of Brodgar stands in a flat landscape between two lochs under enormous skies. None of it is heavily managed. You can touch it. Hidden knowledge, in the most literal sense, lying just beneath the grass.

**Rannoch Moor**
The closest thing to genuine desolation in Britain — a vast, treeless, boggy plateau in the central Highlands, largely roadless, largely unvisited. The train line crosses it; you can get out at Rannoch Station and be standing in something that genuinely does not care about you. The infinite-veil landscape, available without a long-haul flight.

**The Faroe Islands**
Eighteen volcanic islands in the North Atlantic, halfway between Norway and Iceland. Almost no trees. Waterfalls falling directly into the sea. Fog rolling through mountain passes. The grass is an impossible green against black basalt cliffs. A small Norse-speaking culture that has existed in extraordinary isolation. The most otherworldly landscape reachable from the UK — a few hours by plane — and almost nothing about it has been flattened by mass tourism yet.

**Iceland — The Interior**
Not the Blue Lagoon Iceland of package tourism. The Highlands are a lunar desert of black sand and lava fields that feels genuinely pre-human. The geothermal landscape (Landmannalaugar, the obsidian mountains of Hrafntinnusker) has the quality of being somewhere the Earth is still working out what it wants to be. Abiogenesis made landscape. Go in late September when the tourists have left and the northern lights begin.

### Continental Europe — Wilderness

**The Lofoten Islands — Norway**
Mountains rising directly from the sea, no foothills, no transition — the land simply erupts from the water. In deep winter the sun barely rises and the blue hour stretches for hours, a light that is genuinely unlike anything in daytime. The fishing villages are small and old and the landscape overwhelms everything human in it. This is the sublime in the Kantian sense: something that makes you feel the precise limits of the self.

**The Romanian Carpathians — Transylvania**
One of the last genuinely wild landscapes in Europe. Old-growth forests covering the mountain flanks — the largest remaining in the continent — still inhabited by bears, wolves, and lynx. Medieval Saxon villages sitting in valleys where almost nothing has changed since the 13th century. Fortress churches, ancient haymaking traditions, pastoral landscapes that look like paintings of a Europe that elsewhere no longer exists. The simulacrum hasn't arrived yet; the original is still present.

**The Albanian Alps — Valbonë**
The most unknown and dramatic mountain landscape in Europe. The Accursed Mountains (Bjeshkët e Namuna) along the Albanian-Montenegrin border are the Dolomites without the tourists or the infrastructure — steep limestone, deep canyon valleys, traditional guesthouses in villages that only got electricity in recent decades. Genuinely remote and genuinely stunning. Reachable cheaply and easily from the UK; almost nobody goes.

**The Białowieża Forest — Poland / Belarus**
The last primeval lowland forest in Europe — the only place on the continent where the original post-glacial forest cover survives intact. European bison still roam it. The trees are enormous and ancient. It has a quality of density and biological depth that no other European forest has: the silence here is inhabited rather than empty, the way a forest sounds when it has never been cleared. Directly relevant to the abiogenesis interest — this is what the living world looked like before we simplified it.

**The Dolomites — Northern Italy**
Extraordinary pale limestone towers rising above alpine meadows — the rock itself is made of ancient sea creatures, compressed and uplifted, so that what you are climbing is a coral reef from 250 million years ago. The Ladin people (Ladino-speaking, pre-Roman, culturally distinct) still inhabit the valleys. Less wild than the Albanian Alps but more visually overwhelming. The right light — early morning, or storm-approaching — turns the rock pink and then red in a way that has no equivalent. Geology made sublime.

**The Camargue — Southern France**
Where the Rhône meets the Mediterranean: a flat delta of salt marshes, lagoons, and wetland where wild white horses, black bulls, and pink flamingos have coexisted with the same simple human cultures for centuries. A different kind of wilderness — not vertical and overwhelming but horizontal and strange. A landscape that operates at a different timescale and a different logic to everything around it. The light in late afternoon is extraordinary.

**Ordesa y Monte Perdido — Spanish Pyrenees**
The most sublime mountain landscape in Spain. The canyon of Ordesa is a vast, sheer-walled trench cut into limestone, with the plateau of Monte Perdido — the Lost Mountain — rising above it in permanent snow. The name is not accidental: this is landscape as disappearance, as the self reduced to scale. The high route (the Faja de Pelay) traverses the canyon wall at mid-height and gives you the vertiginous sense of being inside something that has no interest in your presence. Less visited than the French Pyrenees, more dramatic, more wild.

**Extremadura — Western Spain**
One of the least visited and wildest regions in Western Europe. The Monfragüe National Park is the best place on the continent to see large birds of prey — griffon vultures, black vultures, Spanish imperial eagles, black storks — in extraordinary numbers, circling above ancient river gorges in a landscape of cork oak and granite. The wider region contains Roman ruins (Mérida has the best-preserved Roman theatre in the world), Visigothic heritage, and medieval hilltop towns where almost nobody goes. A landscape where the historical layers are not managed into a product — they are simply there, accumulating.

**Bardenas Reales — Navarra**
A badlands plateau in the middle of green northern Spain — semi-desert, eroded tufa towers and clay formations, a lunar landscape that looks entirely wrong for its geography. Used as a film location (Game of Thrones among others, which is a reason to go to the original rather than wait for the copy). The light at dusk turns the clay formations amber and then red. Directly adjacent to the Pyrenees and the green Navarrese countryside, which makes the contrast more disorienting — the veil thinning at the border between two completely different worlds.

**The Alpujarras — Sierra Nevada, Andalusia**
The southern slopes of the Sierra Nevada, where a chain of white Moorish villages clings to deep ravines at altitude. The villages (Pampaneira, Bubión, Trevélez) were built by the Moors and survived the Reconquista largely intact — their architecture, their water systems (acequias channelling snowmelt through the terraces), and their way of organising space in relation to the mountain are all pre-Christian. Gerald Brenan lived here in the 1920s and wrote *South from Granada* about it — one of the great books about a place and what it does to the mind. The highest peaks (Mulhacén, Veleta) are the highest in mainland Spain and genuinely wild. Snow in April, desert light by midday.

**Picos de Europa — Asturias / Cantabria**
A compact massif of extraordinary violence — limestone peaks rising almost vertically from the Atlantic coast, no foothills to speak of, the mountains simply arriving out of the green northern plain. The Cares Gorge (Ruta del Cares) traverses twelve kilometres of sheer canyon at river level, walls rising 1,000 metres on either side, a walk that does something to the sense of scale that is hard to describe until you've done it. The area gets very little of the summer tourism that the south draws; in autumn it is largely empty, the beech forests turning gold above the gorge, the peaks already in snow.

**Costa da Morte — Galicia**
The "Coast of Death" — named not for marketing but for the hundreds of shipwrecks along this stretch of Atlantic shoreline where the Iberian peninsula ends in a tangle of granite cliffs and hidden rocks. Cape Finisterre (Fisterra) is where the Camino de Santiago ends: pilgrims have been coming here since before Christianity, to stand at what was literally believed to be the edge of the world, looking west into open ocean. The tradition of burning your boots here, your old clothes, the objects that carried the journey — is old and genuine. Beyond the pilgrimage context, the coast itself is wild and almost unvisited: ancient lighthouses on cliff edges, fishing villages where the Atlantic is not scenic backdrop but the entire fact of life, a quality of grey-green light that is completely unlike anywhere else in Spain.

**The Galician Interior — Castro Culture**
Away from the pilgrimage routes, inland Galicia is one of the most archaeologically layered and mythologically alive landscapes in Europe. The *castros* — Iron Age hillforts, thousands of them, many still unexcavated — dot the landscape. The culture that built them was Celtic-Iberian, and its traces are everywhere: in the Galician language (closer to Portuguese than Spanish, with Celtic phonology underneath), in the bagpipes (*gaita*) still played at every festival, in the *meiga* tradition (witchcraft, herbalism, the speaking of spells) that was never fully suppressed. The landscape is green, granite, perpetually misted. A country that has been inhabited continuously for so long that the past is not archaeology but atmosphere.

**Altamira — Cantabria**
The Paleolithic cave paintings at Altamira were made between 36,000 and 14,000 years ago — bison, horses, and deer rendered in ochre and charcoal on a limestone ceiling with a fluency and naturalism that stopped 19th-century scholars from believing they were genuine, because nothing in their model of human prehistory allowed for art that good, that early. The original cave is now closed to protect the paintings; the replica (the Neocueva) is a faithful reproduction and the surrounding museum is serious. But the real reason to go is the landscape around it — the green Cantabrian hills, the proximity of the Picos, the sense of standing in a place where human consciousness was already doing something that has not been improved upon in 36,000 years. Hidden knowledge in the most literal sense: it was hidden in a cave, and when it was found, it was disbelieved.

### Archaeological & Ancient

**Orkney** *(see above — worth listing twice)*

**Malta — The Megalithic Temples & The Hypogeum**
The Maltese temples (Ħaġar Qim, Mnajdra) are among the oldest freestanding stone structures on Earth, predating Stonehenge by a thousand years. The Hypogeum of Ħal Saflieni is an underground sanctuary carved entirely by hand from the rock — three levels descending into the earth, used for burial and ritual, with acoustics engineered so that a human voice resonates through the entire structure. Only 80 visitors per day are permitted. The experience of being underground in a human-made space from 5,000 years ago, in near-darkness, is unlike anything else accessible in Europe.

**Göbekli Tepe — Turkey**
The oldest known temple complex on Earth, 12,000 years old — predating agriculture, predating writing, predating everything we thought we knew about the sequence of civilisation. Massive carved pillars arranged in circles, depicting animals no one has fully explained. Hidden for millennia under a deliberate mound of earth — someone buried it intentionally. Unearthed only in 1994. If ancient wisdom and hidden knowledge draw you, this is the source: the question of what they knew, and why they wanted to preserve it, has no satisfactory answer.

**Cappadocia — Turkey**
A landscape of volcanic tufa carved by millennia of erosion into towers and chimneys — and then hollowed out from the 4th century onward to create underground cities descending eight levels into the earth, cave churches painted with extraordinary frescoes, entire communities living inside the rock. The landscape above ground is already otherworldly; the landscape beneath it is stranger still.

### Urban — The Right Kind of City

**Tbilisi — Georgia**
An ancient city at the crossroads of every major trade and pilgrimage route between East and West — Persian, Ottoman, Russian, Soviet, and something older beneath all of it. The old town (Abanotubani) is crumbling and beautiful: wooden balconies over cobbled streets, sulphurous bathhouses built over natural hot springs. Georgian wine culture goes back 8,000 years — the oldest in the world. A city that has not yet been processed into a version of itself for consumption. The food is extraordinary; the company tends to be serious.

**Plovdiv — Bulgaria**
One of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in Europe, with a Roman amphitheatre sitting open in the middle of town and a medieval old quarter of painted merchant houses above it. It has the layered quality Sebald finds in Suffolk — the past refusing to stay buried, visible through the present. Quiet, unhurried, cheap, and almost entirely off the tourist circuit. The kind of place where you understand that most of what gets marketed as culture is a simulation of what this actually is.
